# Visual design

## Inputs

Collect the combat camera, battlefield screenshot, character scale, monster scale, project palette, comparable effects, spell descriptions, damage shapes, and timing. Treat competitor effects only as functional references; derive new silhouettes, rhythm, material language, and color structure from the project.

## Design contract

Define each spell with:

- gameplay verb: slash, pierce, fall, erupt, orbit, trap, domain, brand, beam, wave;
- cast origin and ground target;
- motion archetype and direction;
- anticipation, body, impact, and residue duration;
- primary silhouette at gameplay zoom;
- core, midtone, edge, smoke, and residue colors;
- brightest allowed area and maximum white coverage;
- expected screen size and road coverage;
- one sentence describing what a player must read.

## Four-stage storyboard

1. **Anticipation:** communicate target area and direction without obscuring units.
2. **Travel/build:** make the active damaging object unmistakable.
3. **Impact:** place the highest contrast at the ground or foot anchor.
4. **Residue:** confirm the outcome, then clear quickly unless gameplay persists.

Show all four stages over an actual battle background. Evaluate at final device scale, not only on a transparent canvas.

## Readability rules

Judge readability at final gameplay zoom before approving the sheet.

- Preserve one dominant silhouette per spell.
- Separate the damaging body from decorative particles.
- Keep character and monster health bars readable.
- Use white as a narrow core accent, not the full effect body.
- Dark environments need colored midtones; bright environments need dark ink, smoke, or edge contrast.
- Persistent domains need slow internal motion so they never read as a frozen decal.
- Multi-projectile spells need timing or lateral separation so their count is visible.

## Approval output

Deliver a labeled contact sheet containing effect name, key, motion direction, four frames, palette swatches, intended scale, and acceptance notes. Approve the visual language on one representative effect before producing the full family.
